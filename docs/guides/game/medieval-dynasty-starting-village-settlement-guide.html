Build a village that carries itself

A practical route from lonely house to stable workforce: what to build before recruiting, which jobs deserve your first villagers, how to prevent production stalls, and when to expand.

The short answer

Do not recruit merely because someone has good stats. Recruit into a prepared home and a job that replaces a repetitive chore. Your first worker should usually make wood, your second should secure food, and your third should solve whichever bottleneck your chosen playstyle creates.

Storage + spare house→Woodshed worker→Hunting Lodge worker
Villager 1

Lumberjack

Recruit someone with Extraction 3. Logs feed construction, tools, firewood, and later planks, so this worker removes several bottlenecks at once.

Villager 2

Hunter

Recruit someone with Hunting 3. Meat stabilizes food while leather supports bags, waterskins, and sale goods. Keep knives supplied.

Villager 3

Choose the bottleneck

Use Farming 3 for a real crop plan, or another Extraction 3 for stone and limestone. A Water Carrier is comfort, not usually the best third job.

Use skill 3 as the recruitment filter. Recruitable adults begin with skills from 1–3. A level-3 worker produces more immediately, gains mood from a well-matched profession, and trains that skill further. Inspector Mode shows the numbers before you invite them.

Prepare the settlement before the invitation

A recruit needs housing, food, water, and heating material. A job also needs its workplace, a production order, inputs, and tools. Build the small system first; then add the person.

Choose a site for your time, not for worker animations

Before permanent construction

Favor flat building room, nearby water and reeds, convenient roads, enough open ground for fields, and reasonable access to villages or mines. Lumberjacks and hunters produce from their assigned settings regardless of the animation you see. Farmers are the important exception: put the Farm Shed close to the fields it serves.

Build the logistics spine

Before the first recruit

  • Your house and at least one completed spare house.
  • Resource Storage for axes, knives, logs, firewood, and other non-food supplies.
  • Food Storage for food and filled water containers.
  • Woodshed and Hunting Lodge as the first two useful workplaces.
  • Well for filling buckets yourself; it does not need a worker yet.

The two storage buildings employ nobody. Their value is that workplaces can draw inputs and tools from shared storage and deliver their output there.

Stock a buffer

One season of breathing room

Place edible food and filled buckets or waterskins in Food Storage. Put firewood, several axes, several knives, and spare crafting materials in Resource Storage. Check the Management screen’s daily demand rather than relying on a fixed item count: season length, population, insulation, and settings change the answer.

Recruit for a named vacancy

Only after home + job + supplies exist

Inspect travellers, pick the skill that matches the vacancy, invite one, then immediately assign both a house and workplace. Open the workplace’s production tab and set its work percentages. A worker with no assignment produces nothing.

Cheap launch, better later: a basic house is enough to begin. As materials allow, replace wattle with stone, improve the roof, insulate, and decorate. Better housing reduces wood demand and improves mood; it need not delay the first recruit.

Your first four villagers

This order is deliberately conservative. It automates recurring needs before it automates goods that the player can batch-craft in seconds.

No worker needed

Resource Storage

Shared home for raw materials, tools, firewood, and most crafted goods. It feeds workplace inputs and receives non-food output.

No worker needed

Food Storage

Shared home for food and water items. It slows spoilage and allows housed villagers to meet food and drink needs centrally.

Make every assignment complete

“Employed” is not the same as “producing.” Use this five-link check whenever a workplace shows a red icon or its output stays at zero.

Worker+Work order+Tools & inputs

Match the skill

Extraction covers Lumberjacks, Miners, and Water Carriers. Hunting covers Hunters. Farming covers Farmers and Barn Workers. Production covers Cooks, Craftsmen, Seamsters, and Blacksmiths.

2

Assign the actual profession

Give the villager a home and workplace in Management. Recheck assignments after replacing, demolishing, or upgrading buildings.

3

Set production intensity

Open the building’s details and distribute work percentages among products. “No assignments” means the building has no work order.

4

Supply tools and inputs

Put them in Resource Storage or the workplace chest. Examples: axes for logs, knives for meat, buckets for water, and field tools plus seed and fertilizer for crops.

5

Control what villagers consume

In People’s Demand Control, decide which foods, drinks, and wood items residents may use. Once firewood is reliable, disabling logs and planks as heating fuel protects construction stock.

Each season

Rebalance from evidence

Compare daily demand and production, then inspect inventory. If the number is technically positive but the stock keeps falling, an input chain or consumption permission is probably hiding the real constraint.

Housing is also workforce planning

Each house can hold one adult man and one adult woman, plus their children. The pair need not already be married. This makes every housing choice a potential family and labor decision.

If you want growth

Pair compatible ages

An adult man and woman sharing a house may marry and have children. This grows the dynasty naturally and improves family mood, but the timing has a labor cost.

If labor is tight

Delay cohabitation

Use separate houses until another worker can cover the important job. Pregnancy lasts three seasons; after birth, the mother is unavailable for other work for two full years.

Improve gradually

Upgrade the envelope

Stone walls, better roofs, insulation, and decoration improve comfort or mood. Better insulation also reduces the settlement’s wood demand.

Avoid

Recruiting into homelessness

Unhoused villagers cannot properly access shared needs; their mood falls and can eventually reach the point where they leave. Complete the house first.

Practical rule: never let one pregnancy remove the only person covering food or firewood. Before pairing your only Hunter or Lumberjack, train or recruit a backup, or keep enough stock to bridge the absence.

Expand only when the current layer is boring

A healthy settlement feels uneventful: needs stay green, tools last until the next check, and raw stocks rise. That calm is your permission to add another resident or production chain.

Recruitment gate

  • A completed, assignable bed exists.
  • The recruit has a specific job.
  • The workplace is built and has a work order.
  • At least one season of food is buffered.
  • At least one season of water is buffered.
  • Firewood production exceeds current demand.
  • Required tools have spares in Resource Storage.
  • Storage has room for the new output.
  • The recruit’s best skill matches the vacancy.
  • A pregnancy would not break an essential chain.
Stable at 2 workers

Wood + food

You still provide water and batch-craft tools, but the two most repetitive survival loops are automated.

Stable at 4–6 workers

Add material flow

Automate crops or excavation, then water or barn processing. Keep tool-making manual until its repetition justifies a Production worker.

Next layer

Close one supply chain

Choose a goal—bread, clothing, metal tools, animals, or trade—and add only the buildings and workers that support that loop.

Not a goal

Filling every workplace

Empty buildings cost less attention than under-supplied jobs. It is perfectly sensible to use a Workshop or Kitchen yourself long before hiring for it.

Fast troubleshooting

Most early settlement failures are a missing link, not a mysterious simulation.

The worker is assigned but produces nothing

Open the workplace details. Check for a production percentage, then check the red status icon: no assignment, no resource, or no tool. Put the required items in Resource Storage or the workplace chest.

Logs disappear even though the Woodshed is running

Villagers can burn wood-derived items when permitted, and your Lumberjack may also consume logs to make firewood or planks. Increase raw-log allocation, keep a firewood buffer, and use People’s Demand Control to protect logs and planks from heating use.

The village has meat but residents still show no food

Put edible food in Food Storage or individual house chests and confirm it is allowed in demand control. Raw or intermediate items are not automatically equivalent to a permitted edible meal.

The Water Carrier has buckets but water still stalls

The containers must be empty and available to the workplace through shared Resource Storage or its chest; filled water belongs in Food Storage for consumption. Also confirm that water production has a non-zero assignment.

Farmers waste most of the season walking

Build the Farm Shed beside the fields, not beside the village center. Farmers prioritize nearby fields and fieldwork is spatially simulated. Confirm the crop plan, season, tools, fertilizer, and seed before the season begins.

Mood is low even though needs are stocked

Confirm the villager is housed and employed in a job matching a strong skill. Then improve house materials, insulation, and decoration. Family status also affects mood. A job mismatch is survivable, but it is weaker both emotionally and economically.
